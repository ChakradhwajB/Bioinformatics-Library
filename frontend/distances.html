Educational Guide: Sequence Distance Metrics

1. Hamming Distance (Point Mutations)

The Hamming Distance counts the number of positions at which two sequences of equal length differ. In biology, it directly represents point mutations (nucleotide substitutions) without any insertions or deletions. For sequences $S_1$ and $S_2$ comparing up to $L = \min(|S_1|, |S_2|)$, it is formulated as:

$$d_H(S_1, S_2) = \sum_{i=0}^{L-1} \delta(S_1[i], S_2[i]) \quad \text{where } \delta(a,b) = \begin{cases} 0 & a = b \\ 1 & a \neq b \end{cases}$$

2. Levenshtein Distance (Edit Distance)

The Levenshtein Distance measures the minimum number of single-character operations (insertions, deletions, or substitutions) required to transform sequence $S_1$ into $S_2$. Unlike Hamming, Levenshtein distance handles alignments of different lengths. We solve it via dynamic programming:

$$D_{i, j} = \begin{cases} D_{i-1, j-1} & \text{if } S_1[i-1] = S_2[j-1] \\ \min \begin{cases} D_{i-1, j} + 1 & \text{(Deletion)} \\ D_{i, j-1} + 1 & \text{(Insertion)} \\ D_{i-1, j-1} + 1 & \text{(Substitution)} \end{cases} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

3. Algorithmic Complexity Comparison

  • Hamming: Time complexity $\mathcal{O}(L)$ and Space complexity $\mathcal{O}(1)$ as it runs in a single sequential pass without building a 2D matrix.
  • Levenshtein: Time complexity $\mathcal{O}(n \cdot m)$ and Space complexity $\mathcal{O}(n \cdot m)$ to compute and store the 2D grid of subproblems.
