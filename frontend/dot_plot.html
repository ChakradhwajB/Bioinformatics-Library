Educational Guide: Dot Plot Matrices

A Dot Plot is a simple graphical method for comparing two biological sequences. It is a visual representation of the similarity between two sequences, where one sequence is written on the horizontal axis and the other on the vertical axis.

1. Matrix Generation & Rules

The matrix compares all characters of sequence $S_1$ (rows) with sequence $S_2$ (columns). In its simplest form, a dot is plotted at cell $(i, j)$ if: $$S_1[i] = S_2[j]$$ To reduce background noise in long sequences, a sliding window size $w$ and a match threshold $t$ can be specified. A dot is only plotted if there are at least $t$ character matches within the sliding windows starting at $i$ and $j$.

2. Interpreting Dot Plot Patterns

  • Diagonals (Top-Left to Bottom-Right): Represent segments of matching sequences. The main diagonal represents a perfect match (identity).
  • Offset Diagonals: Represent insertions or deletions (indels) between the two sequences.
  • Reverse Diagonals (Top-Right to Bottom-Left): Represent regions of complementarity, palindromes, or inversions.
  • Horizontal/Vertical Lines: Represent highly repetitive sequences (e.g. microsatellites or homopolymeric tracts).

3. Complexity

The algorithm runs in $\mathcal{O}(n \cdot m \cdot w)$ time, where $n = |S_1|$, $m = |S_2|$, and $w$ is the window size. It requires $\mathcal{O}(n \cdot m)$ space to render the DOM matrix visualization elements.
