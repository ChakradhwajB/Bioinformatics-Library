Educational Guide: Central Dogma Transformations

In molecular biology, genetic information is preserved and converted through three main steps described by the Central Dogma: DNA replication, transcription to RNA, and translation to proteins.

1. Transcription (DNA $\rightarrow$ RNA)

Transcription is the process of copying a segment of DNA into RNA. In this translation step, Thymine ($T$) bases are substituted by Uracil ($U$) bases. The sequence is processed linearly in the $5' \rightarrow 3'$ direction.

2. Translation (RNA $\rightarrow$ Protein Peptide)

Translation maps mRNA nucleotides into chains of amino acid residues. This mapping is performed by the ribosome reading triplets of nucleotides called codons. Each codon corresponds to one of $20$ standard amino acids (e.g. $AUG \rightarrow \text{Methionine / Start}$, $UUU \rightarrow \text{Phenylalanine}$). The translation terminates immediately when one of the Stop codons ($UAA$, $UAG$, $UGA$) is reached.

3. Watson-Crick Base Pairing Complementation

Complementary strands are generated using base-pairing rules ($A \leftrightarrow T$, $C \leftrightarrow G$). The reverse complement reverses the complementary strand direction ($3' \rightarrow 5'$ flipped to $5' \rightarrow 3'$), representing the opposing strand of a double helix.
